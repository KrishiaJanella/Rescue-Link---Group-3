VOLCANIC ERUPTION

A volcanic eruption is a geological phenomenon where molten rock, volcanic ash, and gases are expelled from a volcano. This occurs when magma from beneath the Earth's mantle ascends through the crust and reaches the surface. The nature and violence of an eruption depend largely on the magma's composition, the presence of water, and the volcano's structure. There are several types of eruptions, ranging from effusive, where lava flows gently and forms broad, sloping shield volcanoes, to explosive, which are characterized by violent explosions that eject ash, lapilli, and volcanic bombs, creating steep stratovolcanoes. Volcanic eruptions can significantly impact the environment and human communities, altering landscapes, influencing climate through the emission of gases and ash, and posing severe risks to life and property. Thus, understanding and monitoring volcanic activity is essential for predicting eruptions and minimizing their adverse effects.
