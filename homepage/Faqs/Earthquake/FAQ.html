Frequently Asked Questions

Q.   What is an earthquake?

A.   An earthquake is the shaking of the Earth's surface caused by the sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust.


Q.   How do earthquakes occur?

A.   Earthquakes occur when there is a sudden movement along faults in the Earth's crust, resulting in the release of stored energy.


Q.   What causes earthquakes?

A.   Most earthquakes are caused by the movement of tectonic plates, which are large slabs of the Earth's lithosphere that float on the semi-fluid asthenosphere beneath them.


Q.   What is the difference between the focus and epicenter of an earthquake?

A.   The focus is the point within the Earth where an earthquake originates, while the epicenter is the point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus.


Q.   How are earthquakes measured?

A.   Earthquakes are measured using instruments called seismographs, which record the shaking of the Earth's surface. The magnitude of an earthquake is typically measured on the Richter scale or the moment magnitude scale.


Q.   What is the difference between magnitude and intensity in earthquake measurement?

A.   Magnitude measures the energy released at the source of the earthquake, while intensity measures the strength of shaking at a specific location.


Q.   What are aftershocks?

A.   Aftershocks are smaller earthquakes that occur in the same area as the main earthquake, following the main shock. They can continue for days, weeks, or even months after the initial earthquake.


Q.   Can earthquakes trigger tsunamis?

A.   Yes, earthquakes that occur under the ocean floor or near bodies of water can displace large volumes of water, triggering tsunamis.


Q.   How can I stay safe during an earthquake?

A.   Drop, cover, and hold on. Drop to the ground, take cover under a sturdy piece of furniture, and hold on until the shaking stops. If outdoors, move to an open area away from buildings, trees, and utility wires.


Q.   Can earthquakes be predicted?

A.   While scientists can forecast the likelihood of earthquakes occurring in certain areas based on historical data and geological studies, precise prediction of individual earthquakes is currently not possible.


Q.   What should I do after an earthquake?

A.   After an earthquake, check yourself and others for injuries, then assess your surroundings for hazards such as gas leaks or structural damage. Be prepared for aftershocks and follow local authorities' instructions for evacuation or sheltering in place if necessary.
