TSUNAMI

Frequently Asked Questions

Q.   What is a tsunami?

A.   A tsunami is a series of large ocean waves caused by sudden disturbances in the underwater environment, such as earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, landslides, or meteorite impacts.


Q.   What causes tsunamis?

A.   Tsunamis are primarily caused by underwater earthquakes, particularly those that occur along subduction zones where tectonic plates converge and one plate is forced beneath another. Other causes include volcanic activity, landslides, and rare meteorite impacts.


Q.   How fast do tsunamis travel?

A.   Tsunamis can travel at speeds exceeding 500 miles per hour (800 kilometers per hour) in the open ocean, but their speed decreases significantly as they approach shallower coastal waters.


Q.   What are the warning signs of an approaching tsunami?

A.   Warning signs of an approaching tsunami include a sudden and significant receding of the water from the shoreline, a loud roar resembling an oncoming freight train, and the shaking or rumbling associated with an earthquake.


Q.   How can communities prepare for a tsunami?

A.   Communities can prepare for tsunamis by developing and practicing evacuation plans, identifying safe evacuation routes to higher ground, installing tsunami warning systems, educating residents about tsunami risks and safety procedures, and implementing land-use planning and building codes to reduce vulnerability to tsunamis.


Q.   What should I do if a tsunami warning is issued for my area?

A.   If a tsunami warning is issued for your area, immediately move to higher ground or inland away from the coast. Follow evacuation routes designated by local authorities, bring essential supplies such as food, water, and medications, and stay tuned to official sources of information for updates.


Q.   Can tsunamis be predicted?

A.   While earthquakes that generate tsunamis can be detected and monitored using seismographs and ocean buoy systems, accurately predicting the exact timing, size, and impact of a tsunami remains challenging. Early warning systems can provide alerts to at-risk coastal communities, but rapid evacuation is often the most effective response.


Q.   What should I do if I am caught in a tsunami while swimming or boating?

A.   If you are caught in a tsunami while swimming or boating, try to reach the nearest sturdy structure or vessel for shelter and hold on until the waves pass. If unable to reach safety, remain as buoyant as possible and go with the flow of the waves to reduce the risk of injury.


Q.    How long do tsunamis last?

A.   The duration of a tsunami depends on factors such as the size of the initial disturbance and the characteristics of the coastal geography. While individual waves may last for several minutes to hours, the overall impact of a tsunami can persist for days or even weeks as floodwaters recede and recovery efforts begin.


Q.    Are all coastal areas equally at risk of tsunamis?

A.   No, not all coastal areas are equally at risk of tsunamis. Regions located near tectonic plate boundaries, subduction zones, or active volcanic areas are at higher risk, while areas with deep offshore trenches or protective barrier islands may experience reduced tsunami hazard.


Q.    What should I do after a tsunami has passed?

A.   After a tsunami has passed, remain cautious of potential aftershocks, flooding, and other hazards. Only return to coastal areas after authorities have declared them safe, and be prepared for debris, infrastructure damage, contaminated water, and other post-disaster challenges. Follow official guidance and support recovery efforts in affected communities.
