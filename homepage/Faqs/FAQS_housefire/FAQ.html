HOUSE FIRE

Frequently Asked Questions

Q.   What are the common causes of house fires?

A.   Common causes of house fires include cooking accidents, electrical malfunctions, heating equipment failures, smoking materials, candles left unattended, flammable liquids, and arson.


Q.   What should I do if a fire breaks out in my home?

A.   If a fire breaks out in your home, follow your fire escape plan if you have one. Alert other occupants, evacuate the building immediately, call the fire department from a safe location, and do not re-enter the building until it has been declared safe to do so.


Q.   How can I prevent house fires?

A.   Preventive measures to reduce the risk of house fires include installing smoke alarms on every level of your home and inside bedrooms, testing smoke alarms regularly, keeping flammable materials away from heat sources, practicing safe cooking habits, maintaining electrical appliances and wiring, and storing flammable liquids properly.


Q.   What are the most important items to grab during a house fire evacuation?

A.   During a house fire evacuation, prioritize the safety of yourself and others. If time allows and it is safe to do so, grab essential items such as your phone, keys, wallet or purse, important documents (e.g., identification, insurance papers), and any necessary medications.


Q.   How can I make my home more fire-resistant?

A.   Making your home more fire-resistant involves measures such as installing fire-resistant roofing materials, using fire-retardant treatments for curtains, furniture, and other furnishings, ensuring proper clearance around heat sources, and sealing gaps where embers could enter the home.


Q.   What should I do if my clothes catch fire?

A.   If your clothes catch fire, remember to stop, drop, and roll to smother the flames. Cover your face with your hands and roll on the ground until the fire is extinguished. Immediately cool the affected area with water and seek medical attention for any burns.


Q.   How often should I inspect and maintain my smoke alarms?

A.   Smoke alarms should be inspected monthly to ensure they are functioning properly. Test the alarms by pressing the test button, replace batteries at least once a year, and replace smoke alarms every 10 years or as recommended by the manufacturer.


Q.   What should I do if I am unable to escape from a burning building?

A.   If you are unable to escape from a burning building, close all doors between you and the fire to slow its spread, seal gaps around doors and vents with towels or clothing to prevent smoke from entering, and signal for help by waving a flashlight or cloth out the window. Stay low to the ground where the air is less toxic and wait for firefighters to rescue you.


Q.    How can I teach fire safety to my children?

A.   Teach fire safety to children by explaining the importance of smoke alarms, practicing fire drills at home, teaching them how to recognize the sound of a smoke alarm and what to do if they hear it, and discussing fire safety rules such as staying away from matches and lighters.


Q.    What should I do if I suspect a gas leak or smell gas in my home?

A.   If you suspect a gas leak or smell gas in your home, leave the building immediately, avoid using any electrical switches or appliances, do not light matches or use lighters, and call your gas company or emergency services from a safe location outside the building.


Q.    Is it important to have a fire extinguisher in my home, and how should I use it?

A.   Yes, it is important to have a fire extinguisher in your home and to know how to use it properly. Select a multi-purpose fire extinguisher rated for Class A, B, and C fires, keep it in an easily accessible location, and familiarize yourself with the PASS technique: Pull the pin, Aim the nozzle at the base of the fire, Squeeze the handle, and Sweep the nozzle from side to side. Only attempt to extinguish a fire if it is small and contained, and always evacuate if the fire is spreading rapidly or if you are unsure of your ability to extinguish it safely.
