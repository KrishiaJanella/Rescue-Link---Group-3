Frequently Asked Questions

Q.   What is a landslide?

A.   A landslide is the movement of a mass of rock, debris, or earth down a slope. It can be slow or rapid and is typically triggered by factors like heavy rainfall, earthquakes, volcanic activity, or human activities.


Q.   What causes landslides?

A.   Landslides can be caused by various factors, including heavy rainfall, rapid snowmelt, earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, soil erosion, deforestation, construction activities, and land use changes.


Q.   Where do landslides occur?

A.   Landslides can occur in any region with slopes, but they are most common in mountainous or hilly areas. However, they can also occur in coastal areas, river valleys, and even flat terrain under certain conditions.


Q.   How are landslides classified?

A.   Landslides are classified based on their type of movement, such as falls, topples, slides, spreads, flows, or complex movements involving a combination of these types. They can also be categorized by the material involved, like rock, soil, or debris.


Q.   What are the warning signs of a potential landslide?

A.   Warning signs of an impending landslide can include sudden changes in slope angle, tilting of trees or utility poles, new cracks in the ground or structures, increased groundwater levels, and unusual sounds like rumbling or cracking.


Q.   How can we prevent landslides?

A.   Preventing landslides involves various measures such as maintaining vegetation on slopes, constructing retaining walls or barriers, implementing proper drainage systems, avoiding construction on steep slopes, and managing land use effectively.


Q.   What should I do during a landslide?

A.   During a landslide, it's essential to move to higher ground if possible, away from the path of the landslide. If you're indoors, stay there and take cover under a sturdy piece of furniture. If you're outdoors, move quickly to an open area away from the landslide path.


Q.   How can landslides impact communities?

A.   Landslides can have severe impacts on communities, causing loss of life, injuries, destruction of infrastructure, property damage, disruption of transportation routes, loss of agricultural land, and environmental degradation.


Q.    What are some famous historical landslides?

A.   Some famous historical landslides include the Vaiont Dam landslide in Italy in 1963, the Gros Ventre landslide in Wyoming, USA in 1925, the Thistle landslide in Utah, USA in 1983, and the Elm landslide in Switzerland in 1881.


Q.    How are landslides monitored and studied?

A.   Landslides are monitored using various techniques such as satellite imagery, ground-based sensors, inclinometers, ground-penetrating radar, and LiDAR technology. They are also studied through field investigations, laboratory experiments, and numerical modeling.


Q.    What should I do after a landslide?

A.   After a landslide, it's essential to stay away from the affected area until authorities declare it safe. Check for damage to utilities such as gas, water, and electricity, and report any hazards to the appropriate authorities. Assist neighbors who may need help and follow instructions from local emergency management agencies.
